üìñELA Regents Exam Overview

The New York State English Language Arts Regents Examination is a comprehensive assessment that evaluates students' reading, writing, and critical thinking skills. The exam is required of all students enrolled in high schools in the state of New York and consists of three main parts:

üìù Part 1: Reading Comprehension (24 Questions)

Close reading of two to three texts (at least one literature and one informational text), followed by 24 multiple choice questions. Tests your ability to analyze texts, identify themes, understand literary devices, and make inferences.

Time Allocation: 15-20 minutes

‚úçÔ∏è Part 2: Argument Essay (4-5 Texts)

Close reading of four informational texts, with an emphasis on informational texts and may contain graphics. Students compose an essay of argument with a claim based on the sources. Using evidence from at least three of the texts, write a well-developed argument regarding the given topic.

Time Allocation: 40 minutes | Scoring: 6-point rubric √ó 4 = 24 points

üìö Part 3: Text Analysis Essay (1 Text)

Close reading of one informational or literary text and write a two to three paragraph response that identifies a central idea in the text and analyzes how the author's use of one writing strategy develops that central idea.

Time Allocation: 40 minutes | Scoring: 4-point rubric √ó 2 = 8 points

üéØ Graduation Requirements

Students must pass a certain number of regents exams to graduate from public high school. The ELA Regents is required for:

  • Local Diploma: Pass with 55+
  • Regents Diploma: Pass with 65+
  • Advanced Regents Diploma: Pass with 65+
  • Regents with Honors: Average score of 90 or higher on English and 4 other core exams

‚ùìSample Questions & Practice

Reading Comprehension Sample Question:

Based on the passage, the author's primary purpose is to:

A) Entertain readers with an amusing anecdote
B) Describe a historical event in detail
C) Persuade readers to consider a different perspective
D) Provide instructions for a specific procedure
Explanation: The correct answer is C. Look for key words and phrases that indicate the author's intent to influence the reader's opinion, such as "should consider," "it's important to recognize," or persuasive language.
Literary Analysis Sample Question:

The metaphor in line 15 serves to:

A) Create visual imagery
B) Emphasize the character's emotional state
C) Establish the setting
D) Introduce conflict
Explanation: The correct answer is B. Metaphors often reveal deeper meanings about characters' inner experiences. Always consider how literary devices contribute to characterization and theme.
Text Structure Sample Question:

The author organizes paragraph 3 by:

A) Chronological order
B) Spatial order
C) Cause and effect
D) Compare and contrast
Explanation: The correct answer is C. Look for transition words like "because," "therefore," "as a result," or "consequently" that signal cause-and-effect relationships.
Poetry Analysis Sample Question:

In line 12, the phrase "dancing shadows" primarily serves to:

A) Establish the time of day
B) Create a mood of uncertainty
C) Introduce a new character
D) Describe the physical setting
Explanation: The correct answer is B. Personification of "dancing shadows" creates an eerie, uncertain atmosphere. Pay attention to how figurative language contributes to mood and tone in poetry.
Inference Sample Question:

Based on the narrator's actions in lines 23-27, the reader can infer that:

A) The narrator is confident about the future
B) The narrator prefers to work alone
C) The narrator feels conflicted about the decision
D) The narrator lacks experience in the situation
Explanation: The correct answer is C. Look for subtle clues like hesitation, contradictory actions, or internal thoughts that suggest the character's true feelings rather than what they explicitly state.
Central Idea Sample Question:

Which statement best expresses the central idea of the passage?

A) Technology has both benefits and drawbacks
B) Effective communication requires understanding different perspectives
C) Success depends on careful planning and preparation
D) Change is inevitable in modern society
Explanation: The correct answer is B. The central idea encompasses the main message that runs throughout the entire passage. Look for themes that connect all paragraphs, not just details from one section.

üìöComprehensive Answer Guides

üéØ Part 1: Multiple Choice Strategy Guide

Step-by-Step Approach:

  1. Preview Questions: Quickly scan all questions before reading to know what to look for
  2. Active Reading: Underline key phrases, circle transition words, note the main idea
  3. Process of Elimination: Cross out obviously incorrect answers first
  4. Text Evidence: Always refer back to the passage to verify your choice
  5. Final Check: Ensure your answer directly addresses what the question asks
Common Question Types:
  • Main Idea: Look for the central message or thesis
  • Supporting Details: Find specific facts that back up the main idea
  • Author's Purpose: Determine why the author wrote the piece (inform, persuade, entertain)
  • Tone/Mood: Identify the author's attitude or the feeling created
  • Literary Devices: Recognize metaphors, symbolism, irony, etc.

‚úçÔ∏è Part 2: Argument Essay Mastery

5-Paragraph Structure Template:

Introduction Paragraph (5-7 sentences):
  • Hook: Engaging opening statement
  • Context: Brief background on the issue
  • Thesis: Clear position statement with preview of main points
Example Thesis: "While technology offers numerous educational benefits, schools should limit screen time because excessive use harms student health, reduces face-to-face social skills, and decreases attention span."
Body Paragraphs (2-3 paragraphs, 6-8 sentences each):
  • Topic Sentence: Introduces the main point
  • Evidence: Specific examples from provided texts
  • Analysis: Explain how evidence supports your argument
  • Connection: Link back to thesis
Conclusion Paragraph (4-6 sentences):
  • Restate Thesis: In different words
  • Summarize Main Points: Brief recap
  • Call to Action/Broader Implications: Why this matters

    üìäDetailed Scoring Rubrics

    üìù Part 2: Argument Essay (6-Point Rubric)

    Scoring Criteria:

    • 6 points: Exceptional - Clear claim, sophisticated analysis, compelling evidence from 3+ texts
    • 5 points: Effective - Strong claim, solid analysis, relevant evidence from 3 texts
    • 4 points: Adequate - Clear claim, basic analysis, sufficient evidence from 3 texts
    • 3 points: Developing - Unclear claim, superficial analysis, limited evidence
    • 2 points: Inadequate - Weak claim, minimal analysis, insufficient evidence
    • 1 point: Minimal - Little to no analysis, lacks coherent argument
    Key Focus Areas: Content & Analysis, Command of Evidence, Coherence & Organization, Control of Conventions
    üìö Part 3: Text Analysis (4-Point Rubric)

    Scoring Criteria:

    • 4 points: Comprehensive - Clear central idea, sophisticated analysis of writing strategy with specific evidence
    • 3 points: Proficient - Identifies central idea, adequate analysis of strategy with relevant evidence
    • 2 points: Developing - Basic understanding of central idea, superficial analysis with some evidence
    • 1 point: Beginning - Limited understanding, minimal analysis, little to no evidence
    Task Focus: Identify central idea + analyze how ONE writing strategy develops that idea
    üéØ Rubric Quality Indicators

    Four Quality Areas (Both Rubrics):

    • Content & Analysis: Understanding of task, depth of analysis, development of ideas
    • Command of Evidence: Use of textual evidence, integration of sources, accuracy of citations
    • Coherence & Organization: Logical structure, transitions, paragraph development
    • Control of Conventions: Grammar, syntax, vocabulary, sentence structure

    üìöEssential Literary Terms & Devices

    üé≠ Figurative Language
    • Metaphor: Comparison without "like" or "as" (Life is a journey)
    • Simile: Comparison using "like" or "as" (brave as a lion)
    • Personification: Giving human qualities to non-human things
    • Hyperbole: Deliberate exaggeration for effect
    • Irony: Contrast between expectation and reality
    • Symbolism: Objects representing deeper meanings
    üìù Writing Strategies
    • Rhetorical Questions: Questions asked for effect, not answers
    • Repetition: Repeating words/phrases for emphasis
    • Parallel Structure: Similar grammatical patterns
    • Juxtaposition: Placing contrasting ideas side by side
    • Anecdote: Brief personal story as evidence
    • Statistics: Numerical data to support claims
    üé® Tone & Mood
    • Tone: Author's attitude (serious, humorous, critical)
    • Mood: Feeling created for reader (tense, peaceful, eerie)
    • Diction: Word choice affecting tone
    • Syntax: Sentence structure and length
    • Imagery: Vivid descriptive language
    • Allusion: Reference to other works/events
    üìñ Text Organization
    • Chronological: Time order (first, then, finally)
    • Cause and Effect: Showing relationships (because, therefore)
    • Compare/Contrast: Similarities and differences
    • Problem/Solution: Issue and its resolution
    • Spatial: Physical arrangement (above, below, near)
    • Order of Importance: Most to least significant
